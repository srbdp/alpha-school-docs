# Outcomes Claims

> Alpha School's self-reported performance claims include accelerated learning rates (2x-6.5x faster than traditional peers), top national percentile rankings on standardized tests (MAP, SAT, AP), high college matriculation, and exceptional student satisfaction—though these claims have not been independently verified by third-party academic institutions.

## Overview

Alpha School reports impressive performance outcomes across multiple dimensions. According to Alpha, students learn 2-6.5 times faster than peers in traditional settings, with average MAP test growth of 2.6x national norms and top performers achieving up to 6.5x growth. The school claims students consistently score in the top 1-2% nationally on MAP assessments, with school-level results "nearly always in the 99th percentile." Alpha reports high school students achieve an average SAT score of 1470 (with senior class median of 1535, claimed as highest in Texas), 90% earn 4s or 5s on AP exams, and students can master an entire grade level in 20-30 hours versus the traditional 200 hours. Alpha also claims the ability to remediate students who are 2-3 years behind grade level in just 40-90 hours of focused study.

Founder Joe Liemandt has publicly characterized Alpha as "the best-performing academic school in the U.S." and claims the ability to "move students from the bottom 25% to the top 25% in two years." The school's first graduating class (2025) consisted of 12 students, with 11 (91.7%) matriculating to four-year universities including Stanford, Vanderbilt, and UT Austin. Alpha reports that 96% of students say they love school, 40-60% prefer school to vacation, and the high school runs year-round at student request. The organization claims "a decade of data collection and public reporting since 2014" supports these outcomes.

**Critical context**: These claims are self-reported by Alpha School without independent third-party verification from academic institutions or peer-reviewed research. No independent academic organization has audited Alpha's results, methodology, or raw data. Analysts raise several methodological concerns: (1) small sample sizes (one cohort had only 5 students completing both fall and winter MAP tests), (2) no randomized controlled trials or matched comparison groups, (3) significant selection effects (admission requires top 10th percentile/top 3% IQ scores and &#36;40,000+ annual tuition), (4) inability to isolate the platform's contribution from student selection, (5) marketing materials overstate AI capabilities (described by critical reviewers as "turbocharged spreadsheet" rather than generative AI), (6) no longitudinal college success data exists (first class just graduated 2025), and (7) regulatory skepticism evidenced by charter application rejections—notably Pennsylvania's 2025 rejection citing the "untested" AI model. Additionally, the same 2-Hour Learning platform used in Alpha's homeschool offering reportedly yielded only "1x learning growth" (not 2.6x), suggesting the full-school environment (incentives, guides, culture) drives results more than the platform alone.

## Details

### Learning Speed and Growth Claims

Alpha School claims that students "learn twice as fast" as peers in traditional educational settings. According to Alpha, this manifests in two key ways: (1) students can complete an entire year's grade-level math curriculum in just 20-30 hours of instruction versus the traditional 180-200 hours, and (2) students demonstrate MAP test growth rates averaging 2.6 times national norms, with the top two-thirds achieving 3.6x growth and top performers reaching up to 6.5x growth compared to typical student progress.

Alpha also claims rapid remediation timelines. According to the school, a student who is two years behind grade level can reach proficiency in approximately 40-60 hours of focused study, while a student three years behind requires about 60-90 hours. Alpha reports that "a full grade level requires about 20 hours to master" under their model.

These claims are based on Alpha's internal platform tracking (time-on-task data) and comparisons to NWEA MAP test growth norms. However, several methodological limitations apply: the platform time measurements don't account for additional afternoon academic hours students spend at Alpha; potential teaching-to-test effects have not been ruled out; and there are no independent studies with control groups to verify whether the accelerated timelines reflect the educational method, student selection, or other confounding factors. Notably, Alpha's homeschool platform—using the same 2-Hour Learning software—reportedly yielded only 1x learning growth (not 2.6x), according to independent analyst Scott Alexander of Astral Codex Ten. This suggests that the full school environment (incentives, guides, peer culture) may drive results more than the platform alone.

### Standardized Test Performance Claims

Alpha School claims students consistently perform in the "top 1% nationally" on NWEA MAP assessments, with school-level results "nearly always in the 99th percentile." Co-founder MacKenzie Price has publicly stated the school's classes are "in the top 2% in the country" for learning performance. The Austin Scholar Substack reported that Alpha's K-2 students achieve "top 0.1% national performance" with "100% of students meeting projected RIT scores," while grades 3-6 students perform "typically associated with 2-3 grade levels above their peers."

According to Alpha, students don't just perform well on a single test administration—they demonstrate exceptional growth rates. The school claims average MAP growth of 2.6 times national norms, with top performers reaching 6.5 times typical growth. Alpha reports that K-2 students achieved math growth rates of 32-33 RIT points, "doubling national projections," and cites specific examples such as a 4th grader achieving a math MAP score matching typical 7th-grade performance.

However, critical verification gaps exist. Alpha has not published raw MAP scores, RIT distributions, percentile breakdowns by grade level, or demographic data. NWEA (the organization administering MAP tests) has not independently confirmed Alpha's percentile rankings or growth multiplier calculations. Independent analyst Scott Alexander noted that one cohort had only 5 students who completed both fall 2024 and winter 2025 MAP tests, making sample sizes too small for statistical confidence. He characterized the evidence base as "largely unverified" and noted that "the absurdity of those numbers makes me think that rate will not hold." Additionally, there are no comparison data from other elite schools with similar student populations, making it impossible to determine whether outcomes reflect Alpha's educational approach or pre-existing student capabilities driven by selective admission requirements (top 10th percentile/top 3% IQ).

### High School Academic Achievement Claims

Alpha School reports that its high school students achieve exceptional standardized test scores. According to Alpha, the average SAT score is 1470, with the senior class median reaching 1535—which Alpha characterizes as the "highest in Texas" based on reporting by sympathetic source Austin Scholar. Alpha claims that 90% of students earn scores of 4 or 5 on AP exams, and the school's first graduating class saw 50% of students earn National Merit Scholar or Commended Scholar status. Five students from that class reportedly achieved AP Scholar with Distinction recognition. Individual student examples cited by Alpha include multiple students scoring 790 on the SAT Math section.

These scores, if accurate, would place Alpha students well above national averages (the national SAT average is approximately 1050, while a 1470 represents roughly the 98th percentile). Alpha presents these outcomes as evidence of superior academic preparation enabled by the 2-Hour Learning model.

However, these claims are based on Alpha's internal reporting and have not been independently audited. While College Board scores are verifiable for individual students, aggregate school-level data has not been published or independently confirmed by external evaluators. Additionally, significant selection effects may explain outcomes: Alpha's admission requirements (top 10th percentile or top 3% IQ scores) and high tuition (&#36;40,000+ annually) create a pre-selected student population that may have achieved strong academic outcomes regardless of educational method. No comparison data exists from demographically similar students in traditional settings to isolate Alpha's contribution to these achievements.

### College Outcomes and First Graduating Class

Alpha School's first graduating class in 2025 consisted of 12 students. According to Alpha, 11 of these 12 graduates (91.7%) enrolled in four-year universities, including highly selective institutions: Stanford, Vanderbilt, University of Southern California (USC), Northeastern, Texas A&M, and University of Texas at Austin. Alpha reports that 6 students from this class (50%) achieved National Merit Scholar or Commended Scholar recognition. Founder Joe Liemandt has stated that students who have been in the Alpha system for "a couple of years consistently rank in the top 1% nationally."

Alpha presents these college outcomes as evidence of the school's effectiveness in preparing students for higher education. The high matriculation rate to four-year institutions and representation at selective universities suggests strong academic preparation and college readiness.

However, important limitations apply. This was Alpha's first graduating class, so no longitudinal data exists on college retention rates, GPAs, graduation rates, or career outcomes. The first class graduated in 2025, meaning no multi-year college success data is available to verify whether Alpha students sustain strong performance through college completion. Additionally, college acceptance letters are verifiable for individuals but the aggregate data has not been independently confirmed or audited. The same selection effects that apply to other outcomes (admission requirements, high tuition filtering for motivated high-achieving families) make it difficult to isolate Alpha's contribution from pre-existing student capabilities and family resources.

### Student Satisfaction and Engagement Claims

Alpha School claims exceptionally high student satisfaction rates. According to founder Joe Liemandt, 96% of Alpha students report that they "love school." Alpha further claims that 40-60% of students prefer school to vacation, and that Alpha High School runs year-round at student request—an unusual arrangement that Alpha presents as evidence of genuine student engagement rather than compliance.

Student testimonials support these satisfaction claims. Elle Kristine, an Alpha School junior, stated in a Fox News interview: "I have a lot of friends at traditional school...they're so stressed out, and they're just miserable." Marshall, a 9-year-old Alpha student, told FOX 7 Austin: "If you get a question wrong, [the app] gives you an explanation of how to get it correct for the next question. And that helps you." Parent Scott Jensen stated: "It's working. It's working for all the kids at the school. There's no one that it isn't working for."

However, critical perspective is also available. An anonymous parent who moved their family to Austin specifically to test Alpha (and whose review was featured in Astral Codex Ten) provided a more nuanced assessment. While confirming their children were "marching through and mastering material roughly three times faster than their age-matched peers" and calling the progress "genuine," this parent clarified several ways Alpha's marketing overstates reality: actual academic engagement is 3-4 hours (not 2 hours as marketed), the platform is "not AI-driven as marketed" but rather functions as a "turbocharged spreadsheet checklist," and the "bundle approach" (platform + incentives + culture) matters more than any single element. This reviewer estimated the Alpha approach could benefit only 30-70% of children, not all students universally.

No systematic satisfaction survey data has been published by Alpha, including methodology, response rates, or longitudinal tracking. Testimonials may reflect selection bias, as dissatisfied families who left Alpha are not represented in public statements. Additionally, the high tuition and selective admission create a population of families already committed to and invested in the school's success, potentially inflating satisfaction ratings compared to a more diverse population.

### Claims About School-Level Performance

Founder Joe Liemandt has made sweeping claims about Alpha School's overall academic standing. In a Colossus podcast interview, Liemandt characterized Alpha as "the best-performing academic school in the U.S." He has also claimed the ability to "move students from the bottom 25% to the top 25% in two years" through Alpha's approach. The school asserts "a decade of data collection and public reporting since 2014" supports these performance claims.

The Austin Scholar Substack reported that multiple AI systems analyzed Alpha's data and concluded the school performs in the "top 1% of districts nationally," with growth metrics surpassing elite private schools. However, the Austin Scholar author explicitly cautioned that this data represents "data outflow of an engineering project, rather than research data" and noted it "lacks independent third-party verification or peer-reviewed research validation."

Critical limitations apply to these school-level performance claims. Detailed quantitative data, raw scores, sample sizes, demographic breakdowns, and methodology are not publicly available, making independent verification impossible. While Alpha claims a decade of data, comprehensive datasets have not been published or made available to external researchers.

Regulatory skepticism suggests these claims are not universally accepted. Alpha has faced charter school application rejections, most notably in Pennsylvania in 2025 where the Pennsylvania Department of Education rejected the application, citing the school's "untested" artificial intelligence instructional model. This regulatory pushback indicates that educational authorities reviewing Alpha's claims in detail have not been convinced the model is sufficiently proven for public charter authorization.

Additionally, independent analyst Frank Hecker noted a significant gap between Alpha's marketing and operational reality. Founder Liemandt clarified in interviews that "We literally don't have chat functionality activated in our AI...It's not a chatbot," contradicting marketing materials that emphasize AI-powered personalized learning. Hecker argues this creates "misleading expectations about ChatGPT-style tutoring" and represents a marketing-versus-reality gap that undermines Alpha's broader claims.

## Examples

### Example 1: Learning Speed Claim - Grade-Level Mastery

**Scenario**: A 4th grade student at Alpha School completes a full year's math curriculum.

According to Alpha School's claims, the student progresses through multiplication, division, fractions, decimals, and geometry at their own pace using the 2-Hour Learning platform. The student completes the entire grade level in 20-30 hours of instruction time versus the traditional 180-200 hours required in conventional classrooms. The student must demonstrate 90%+ mastery on each unit assessment before advancing to the next concept. Alpha attributes this accelerated timeline to AI-powered adaptive learning that provides immediate feedback and adjusts difficulty based on student performance, eliminating wasted time on already-mastered concepts.

**VERIFICATION STATUS**: This claim is self-reported by Alpha School based on internal platform data tracking time-on-task. No independent study has verified this timeline or methodology. Potential confounding factors include selection effects (students admitted to Alpha already score in top 10th percentile or demonstrate top 3% IQ), possible teaching-to-platform-assessments effects, and lack of control groups comparing similar students in traditional settings. The platform time measurement does not account for additional afternoon hours students spend on academic activities at Alpha. Additionally, Alpha's homeschool platform using identical software reportedly achieved only 1x learning growth (not the claimed 2x-6.5x), suggesting the full school environment (guides, incentives, peer culture) drives results more than the platform itself.

### Example 2: Standardized Test Performance - MAP Growth

**Scenario**: An Alpha student takes the NWEA MAP Reading assessment in fall and spring.

According to Alpha School's reporting, the student begins the fall term with a MAP Reading RIT score of 185, placing them at approximately the 50th percentile for their grade level. By spring, after a school year using the 2-Hour Learning model, the student achieves a MAP Reading RIT score of 201—a 16-point gain. Alpha compares this to national growth norms (typical students gain approximately 6 RIT points in a year) and reports this represents 2.6x growth compared to peers. The school reports in mid-year updates that the student's performance places them in the "99th percentile" ranking nationally. Alpha attributes this exceptional growth to personalized AI-driven instruction, mastery-based progression, and the efficiency of the 2-Hour Learning model.

**VERIFICATION STATUS**: NWEA MAP testing is independently administered and nationally normed, providing a legitimate standardized assessment framework. However, Alpha's interpretation of results—specifically the growth multiplier calculations (2.6x, 6.5x) and percentile rankings—are self-reported and have not been independently confirmed by NWEA or external researchers. Raw data including individual student scores, RIT distributions, grade-level breakdowns, sample sizes, and demographic information are not publicly available. Independent analyst Scott Alexander noted that one cohort had only 5 students who completed both fall and winter tests, indicating very small sample sizes that preclude statistical confidence. NWEA has not published any independent analysis confirming Alpha's percentile claims or growth rate calculations. No comparison data exists from other elite schools with similar student populations, making it impossible to determine whether results reflect Alpha's educational model or student selection (admission requires top 10th percentile/top 3% IQ scores).

### Example 3: College Outcome - First Graduating Class

**Scenario**: Alpha School's first graduating class completes high school in 2025.

According to Alpha School, the graduating class consisted of 12 students. Of these, 11 students (91.7%) enrolled in four-year universities, including highly selective institutions: Stanford University, Vanderbilt University, University of Southern California, University of Texas at Austin, Texas A&M University, and Northeastern University. Additionally, 6 students from this class (50%) achieved National Merit Scholar or Commended Scholar recognition—a rate significantly higher than the national average of approximately 3-4%. Alpha presents these college outcomes as evidence of superior college preparation enabled by the 2-Hour Learning model and the school's mastery-based approach to education.

**VERIFICATION STATUS**: College acceptance letters are verifiable for individual students, but the aggregate data has not been independently audited or confirmed by external organizations. The first class graduated in 2025, so no multi-year longitudinal data exists on college retention rates, GPAs, graduation rates, or career outcomes. It is too early to assess whether Alpha graduates sustain strong performance through college completion and beyond. Significant selection effects apply: Alpha's admission requirements (top 10th percentile or top 3% IQ scores) and high tuition (&#36;40,000+ annually) create a pre-selected population of high-achieving, well-resourced students who may have achieved strong college outcomes regardless of high school attended. No comparison data exists from demographically similar cohorts in traditional educational settings to isolate Alpha's specific contribution to college matriculation success.
